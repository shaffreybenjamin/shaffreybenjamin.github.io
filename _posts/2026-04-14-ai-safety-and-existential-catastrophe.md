title: "AI Safety and Existential Catastrophe"
date: 2026-04-15

In his book the precipice, Toby Ord differentiates between two types of existential catastrophes; that of human extinction and that of the permanent unrecoverable loss of our potential. There are many persuasive arguments for being concerned about the first of these two types. The book "If Anyone Builds It Everyone Dies" by Eliezer Yudkowsky and Nate Soares is an accessible and well-written example. 

I do find it alarming that the top AI companies are currently racing towards superintelligence and I am enthusiastic that the field of AI safety is an active and growing area of research. However, I find myself more concerned about the latter of these types given recent events and how societies are responding to this transformative technology.

There are many ways in which this permanent unrecoverable loss of our potential could manifest but amongst them are a global totalitarian regime or a global network of autocracies that assist each other in remaining in power and in further consolidating power. This latter example is already reministcent of our present-day world, as the historian and journalist Anne Applebaum has written extensively about, particularly in her book Autocracy Inc.

In AI safety there is an important ongoing area of research into aligning artificially intelligent systems with the intentions of the engineers that grow them. Setting aside the highly significant philopsphical problem of whether the outcome is good for conscious creatures if powerful systems do what a small number of engineers at the current large AI labs want, this is an enormously difficult technical challenge. We are far from a solution for it.

The problem can be stated in general terms; how do you align powerful entities with the values and wishes of less powerful ones? There is an important connection here between these two different types of existential catastrophes and our efforts to prevent them; governments and corporations are powerful entities, early forms of superintelligence we could say. So we could rephrase the question above for these entities too, how do we align governments and corporations with the wellbeing and interests of society and even conscious creatures in general?

I do not think that the task of aligning a superintelligent AI and a corporation are entirely different problems and it strikes me as plausible that progress in one of these areas can shed light on ways of making progress in the other. And it may be that there are important fundamental scientific questions that we must begin to answer in order to make such progress. 

For example, we currently think of intelligence as a phenomenon that is localisable to a biological brain. This framing does have enormous explanatory power and yet it does not seem to be a fully satisfactory explanation. If this really were the case then we would expect to find features of human brains as distinct from other similarly intelligent non-human animals that give a compelling account of why we are the dominant species on this planet. There are important differences to be sure, but not especially salient structural ones; we do not have the biggest brains, nor the most complex neural wiring, nor special brain areas only and exclusively found in humans.

But there is a way in which we are special, something that sets us apart from non-human animals; namely our capacity for language. This serves as the basis of our power. We can cooperate in large groups because of language and as a result our intelligence is of a general kind because of this ability. This strongly suggests to me that intelligence should not be exclusively thought of as an individual phenomenon but can also be usefully thought of as a group-level phenomenon. So too can we usefully think of stupidity as both a group-level as well as an individual level phenomenon.

I think it's clear to many that the current incentive structures that dominate in our social world would not be associated with any measure of the group that would score highly in intelligence. We have this claim repeated again and again that markets are the most efficient integrators of information, i.e. human preferences that we have yet discovered in the social world, and yet it is clear that this is not in reality what is happening, however plausible it may sound in theory.
